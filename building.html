Building Your PC

Now that you know about the parts that go into your computer, it's time to build! Below is a step-by-step guide to walk you through each part of the process.

Step One: Getting started

First, you'll want to make sure you have some tools ready. You'll need screwdrivers, a thermal paste applicator if your CPU cooler doesn't already have thermal paste applied, zip/cable ties to secure and organize cables and something to cut them with, pliers to hold screws and help grip parts. You'll also want a small container to hold the screws so they don't get lost. It can also be helpful to have access to a first aid kit nearby just in case of any accidents. Make sure you ground yourself and your work area before starting to prevent any static electricity. You could get an anti-static wrist strap to help with this. Clear your workspace and make sure you have enough room to start building. You'll want a well lit area so you can see what you're doing clearly. A headlamp or flashlight will also be helpful for this.

Step Two: Installing the Motherboard and CPU

Place your motherboard on top of a clean, dry, anti-static surface. Take a look at what socket type your CPU has. This is important because you don't want to bend or break the pins or latch that will hold the CPU in place. Once you do that, remove the plastic cover socket that reveals the slot for the CPU. Line up the CPU with the socket. There should be an indicator on which direction to place it. Once the CPU is in place, make sure it is secure and then close the retention mechanism and push down the latch. Be extremely gentle with the CPU and make sure you don't push or force it into place.

Step Three: Installing the RAM

You'll want to check the motherboard manufacturer guide to know which slots to place the RAM into. There will be labels on the motherboard that indicates what each slot is connected to. Leaving the slots closest to the CPU open improves stability and speed. Push down the tabs at the end of the slot where you plan on installing the RAM. Look for the notch on the stick and line it up with the matching post then line up the grooves in the slot. It is easier to line up one side at a time. Next, you'll want to push the RAM into place until you feel resistance and then keep pushing until the tabs at the end of the slot push back into place. Be careful not to be too forceful. If it is done properly, you'll hear a click when the tabs lock back into place. Repeat this process with any other sticks you plan on installing.

Step Four: Installing the SSD

You can take a look at the manual for the motherboard to see where the SSD slots are if you aren't able to find them. You'll want to unscrew the phillips screw on the standoff (if applicable). There may also be a standoff included in the box with your motherboard that you'll have to twist into place on the 2280 position. There is also a 2260 and 2242 slot you could place the standoff in depending on the size of your card. Insert the pin side of the SSD into the slot at a little bit of an angle and then lower the back of the drive onto the standoff and screw it into place.

Step Five: Installing the CPU cooler

To install the CPU cooler,start by installing the backplate. To do so, lift the motherboard carefully and line up the backplate with the four holes on the back. Your backplate may or may not have screws with them. Next up is installing the mounting brackets. Start by removing all the fans off the cooler. You'll need to screw in the mounting brackets either on the sides of the cooler or onto the motherboard. If your CPU cooler doesn't have thermal paste already applied, your cooler should include a tube of it. Either way make sure there is thermal paste applied to the CPU before making contact with it. After placing down the cooler, apply a small amount of pressure to help spread the thermal paste.

Step Six: Prepare the Case and Install the Motherboard

Now is a good time to start preparing to put your motherboard in the case. Before you start, it may be helpful to place your case on top of a blanket or towel to avoid scratching the PC case or whatever surface you are working on. Place the side panels of the case somewhere safe like back in the box to avoid breaking them. Once the side panels are off, install the I/O shield if you have one. Line it up to the cutout on the back of the case and snap it into place.

To begin installing the motherboard into the case, start by installing the standoffs. There will be screw holes in the motherboard tray inside the case. You'll want to match these holes with the ones on your motherboard and screw in the standoffs. Next, carefully place the motherboard into the case making sure it matches with the ports near the I/O shield and onto the standoffs. After that, secure the motherboard by using the screws provided. Make sure the screws are tightened evenly and avoid over tightening to prevent damage. You can now connect your cables for the CPU known as EPS connectors. You'll find these near the top left of the motherboard but refer to your motherboard's manual if you're unable to find them.

After that, it's time to attach the front panel connectors which are made up of the power and reset switches, power LED and HDD LED, and the front USB and audio. Refer to your manual on where you can find the pin layout. Finally, you'll want to connect the SATA cables for your SSD. You should also connect any case fans to the motherboard's fan headers at this time.

Step Seven: Installing the PSU

It is now time to connect the power supply unit. You'll need to find the mounting space for it which is usually near the top-rear or bottom of the case. Once you find it, slide the PSU into place and secure it using screws. You'll need to figure out which cables you'll be using. You'll need the ATX, EPS, PCI and any SATA cables for things such as drives or fans. Make sure the PSU is switched off and don't plug it into the wall until you finish installing and double-checking everything.

Step Eight: Installing the GPU

Look for the PCIe slot on your motherboard, usually located around the CPU. Remove the metal brackets that will reveal the GPU ports. Next, insert the GPU by aligning the gold connector on the GPU with the open PCIe slot. Push down gently until the card is in place. Screw in the GPU using the screws from the metal brackets. This will secure it in place. Now you'll want to connect the PCI cable from the PSU. Do a final check to make sure everything is secured properly.

Now is a good time to check over everything and manage your cables before turning on the computer. Make sure everything is in place properly and double check that there are no leftover screws or anything unnecessary still in the case.

Step Nine: Setting Up the Operating System

At last, it is finally time to start setting up the operating system. For this guide, I will be explaining how to install Windows. After installing the GPU, plug in your monitor, keyboard and mouse. You'll need a USB flash drive and a valid Windows license. You'll need to download the Media Creation Tool and create installation media for the USB flash drive. Complete the setup. Once you're done, insert the flash drive into the new computer. Turn it on and press the key for boot options according to your motherboard manual. Select the USB and once it loads, hit "Install Now". Enter the activation code and select which version you'll be using. Then you'll want to accept the license agreement and install Windows.

Congratulations! You built your new computer. Make sure everything is up to date and install your drivers. You can choose to use Microsoft Edge or install whichever web browser you prefer. Now it's time to personalize your computer and optimize it for your needs!
